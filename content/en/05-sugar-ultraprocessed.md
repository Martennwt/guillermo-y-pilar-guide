# Sugar & "Factory Food" 🚨 (the eye-opener)

This is the module that changes how you walk through a supermarket. We'll keep it honest — not scary
for the sake of scary — but the facts here are genuinely important, and they're backed by serious
science.

## Part 1 — Sugar and your brain

Here's a real experiment people find hard to believe.

> In a 2007 study, scientists gave rats a choice between **sweet water** and **intravenous cocaine** —
> one of the most addictive drugs there is. **Almost every rat chose the sugar.** Even rats already
> hooked on cocaine preferred the sweet taste.
> *(Lenoir, Cantin, Serre, Ahmed — "Intense Sweetness Surpasses Cocaine Reward", PLoS ONE 2007.)*

What does this mean for you? Not that "sugar is a drug" in a literal, scary sense — it's a rat study,
and humans are more complicated. But it shows something true and useful:

**Sugar lights up the brain's reward system very powerfully.** Our sweet-taste was built for a world
where sugar was rare (a bit of ripe fruit, some honey). Modern food floods that system with
super-sized sweetness it was never designed for. That's *why* "just one bite" is so hard, why you can
keep eating sweets long after you're full, and why cutting back feels like withdrawal for a few days.

**Good news:** that pull *fades fast.* After a week or two of less sugar, your cravings genuinely
shrink and fruit starts tasting sweet again. The first few days are the hardest — then it gets easy.

### How much sugar is "too much"? (WHO)

The World Health Organization recommends keeping **free sugars** (sugar added to food + the sugar in
honey, syrups, and fruit juice) under **10% of your daily calories — about 50 g/day** for an average
adult. Better still: **under 5%, about 25 g (≈6 teaspoons) per day.**

For perspective: **one can of cola ≈ 35 g of sugar.** A single can can blow most of your day's
budget. This is why *drinks* are the first place to look.

## Part 2 — Where sugar hides

You expect it in cake and cola. The sneaky part is the "savory" and "healthy-looking" stuff:

- Fruit juices and smoothies from a carton, flavored yogurts, breakfast cereals and granola
- Ketchup, BBQ and tomato sauces, many "light" / low-fat products (sugar replaces the fat)
- Bread and pastries, "energy" and "protein" bars, sweetened coffees

> Habit: **read the label, find "of which sugars."** You'll be shocked how often it's high in things
> that don't taste sweet. Awareness alone changes what lands in your basket.

## Part 3 — Industrial fats (not all fat — *these* fats)

Fat itself is not the enemy (olive oil, fish, nuts, avocado are great). The problem is **industrial /
trans fats** and a flood of cheap refined oils in fried and packaged food.

- **Industrial trans fats** (in some margarines, fried fast food, packaged baked goods, "partially
  hydrogenated oils") raise "bad" LDL cholesterol, lower "good" HDL, and clearly **increase heart
  disease risk.** The WHO is trying to eliminate them worldwide — they estimate trans fats cause
  **~500,000 premature deaths a year.** There is *no safe amount.*
- **Deep-fried, high-temperature starchy foods** (potato chips, fries) also form **acrylamide**, a
  compound classed as *probably carcinogenic to humans.* The crispier/darker and more industrial,
  the more there tends to be.

## Part 4 — Ultra-processed food (the big picture)

Scientists sort food into groups. The one to watch is **ultra-processed food (UPF)**: things made
mostly in a factory from cheap ingredients, additives, colors, and flavors — stuff you couldn't make
in your own kitchen. Think chips, packaged sweets and pastries, instant noodles, sugary drinks,
processed meats, many "ready meals."

Large studies links **diets high in ultra-processed food** with higher risk of **obesity, type-2
diabetes, heart disease, some cancers, and earlier death.** And in a tightly controlled NIH study,
people given an ultra-processed diet **ate ~500 more calories a day** and gained weight — eating the
*same* offered calories' worth — compared to a whole-food diet. The food itself nudges you to overeat.

### A special note on processed meat

The WHO's cancer agency (IARC) classifies **processed meat** (sausages, bacon, ham, hot dogs,
chorizo) as a **Group 1 carcinogen** — the same *confidence* category as tobacco smoke (this means
the evidence it can cause cancer is strong, **not** that a sausage is as dangerous as a cigarette).
Eating ~50 g of processed meat a day is linked with about an **18% higher risk of colorectal
cancer.** **Red meat** is "probably carcinogenic" (Group 2A). Translation: enjoy them
*occasionally*, not daily.

## The takeaway (one line)

> **The closer a food is to how it grew, the better. The more a factory had to do to make it, the
> less it loves you back.**

You don't have to be perfect or paranoid. Just shift the *balance* of your week toward real food, and
treat sugar and ultra-processed stuff as the occasional guest — not the daily staple. The next module
makes this visual and easy.

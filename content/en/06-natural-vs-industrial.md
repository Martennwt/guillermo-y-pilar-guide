# Real Food vs. Factory Food 🍎🏭 (the cheat sheet)

One question cuts through almost every food decision:

> **"How close is this to the way it grew — and how much did a factory have to do to make it?"**

The more steps, additives, and machines between the field and your plate, the more careful you want
to be. Here's the side-by-side. In the interactive version, **hover any row** to see the swap and
the *why*.

## The comparison table

| 🍎 Closer to nature (eat freely) | 🏭 More factory (treat as a guest) | Why it matters (on hover) |
|---|---|---|
| Whole oats you cook & chew | Sugary breakfast cereal / oat shake | Whole + chewed = full for hours; processed/liquid = spike then hungry |
| Fresh or frozen vegetables | Vegetable "crisps" / fried snacks | Same plant, but fried adds oil, salt, acrylamide, and loads of calories |
| Whole fruit (apple, orange) | Fruit juice / fruit-flavored drink | Juice = sugar without the fiber; whole fruit fills you and feeds you |
| Plain yogurt + fresh fruit | Flavored "fruit" yogurt | Flavored ones often hide 2–4 teaspoons of added sugar per pot |
| Fresh fish or chicken | Breaded nuggets / fish fingers / sausages | Processing adds breadcrumb, oil, salt, additives — and the cancer-risk of processed meat |
| Water, coffee, tea, sparkling water | Cola, energy drinks, sweet iced tea | One can ≈ a day's sugar budget, and liquids don't fill you up |
| Olive oil (measured) | Margarine / "vegetable spread" / fried-food oil | Olive oil is heart-healthy; industrial/trans fats harm the heart |
| Potatoes (boiled/baked) | Potato chips / fries | Same potato — frying multiplies the calories ~4–5× and adds acrylamide |
| Nuts (a small handful) | Chocolate bar / pastry | Nuts: protein, fiber, good fats, filling. Bar: sugar hit, then crash |
| Beans, lentils, chickpeas | Instant noodles / packaged ready-meal | Legumes: cheap, filling, high fiber & protein. Ready-meals: salt, additives, low fullness |
| Homemade stew or grilled fish | Frozen pizza / fast food | You control the oil, salt, and portion when *you* cook |
| A square of dark chocolate (85%) | A whole milk-chocolate bar | A small bit of dark = real treat, low sugar; the bar = sugar bomb |

## How to read a label in 10 seconds

When you do buy something packaged, glance at three things:

1. **Ingredient list length.** Short list of words you recognize = good sign. A paragraph of
   chemicals, colors, and syrups = ultra-processed.
2. **"of which sugars."** Lower is better. Remember the day's budget is ~25–50 g *total*.
3. **The fat type.** Avoid "partially hydrogenated" oils (trans fats). Olive oil good; mystery
   "vegetable fat" in fried/baked goods, be wary.

> A balanced note on "E-numbers": not every additive is bad — some are harmless (E300 is just vitamin
> C). The real signal isn't the letter E; it's **how processed the whole product is.** A short
> ingredient list of real foods beats a long list every time.

## The simple rule of thumb

You don't need to memorize the table. Just ask the one question at the top:

> **Could my grandmother recognize this as food? Could I roughly make it in my own kitchen?**

If yes → eat freely. If it's a factory invention of sugar, refined oil, and additives → enjoy it
*occasionally*, on purpose, as a guest. Not the daily staple.

# Eat MORE, Weigh LESS 🥗 (the volume trick)

This is the most fun idea in the whole guide, and once you see it you can't un-see it.

> **Two foods can have the same calories — and one keeps you full for 4 hours while the other is
> gone in 6 bites.**

## The famous comparison

| | A big salad bowl | A chocolate bar |
|---|---|---|
| Example | Lettuce, tomato, cucumber, peppers, 2 boiled eggs, a tin of tuna, a spoon of olive oil | One Snickers |
| Calories | ~350 kcal | ~250 kcal |
| Volume | Fills a huge bowl 🥗 | Fits in your palm |
| How long it keeps you full | Hours — protein + fiber + water + chewing | An hour, then a craving |
| Chewing | Lots | Almost none |

The salad has **more food, more nutrients, more fullness — and fewer-to-similar calories.** The
candy bar gives you a quick hit and leaves you hungry (and wanting another). Same "cost," totally
different value.

## Why this works: energy density

**Energy density** = how many calories are packed into each bite.

- **Low-density foods** (vegetables, fruit, soups, beans, potatoes, lean protein) are mostly water
  and fiber. You can eat a *big satisfying amount* for few calories.
- **High-density foods** (oil, butter, chips, chocolate, pastries, cheese) pack a lot of calories
  into a tiny bite. Easy to overeat without noticing.

You don't have to eat less *food*. You can eat **more** food — you just shift toward the foods that
give you volume per calorie. Your stomach feels full, your brain feels satisfied, and the calorie
math quietly works in your favor.

## The four things that make food filling

1. **Protein** — the #1 most filling macronutrient (eggs, fish, meat, yogurt, legumes).
2. **Fiber** — vegetables, fruit, beans, whole grains. Adds bulk and slows digestion.
3. **Water in the food** — soups, stews, fruit, vegetables fill your stomach with near-zero calories.
4. **Chewing & time** — solid food you chew tells your brain "we're eating" far better than liquids.

A meal with all four (say, a vegetable + bean stew with fish, or a big salad with eggs and tuna)
keeps you full for hours on modest calories. That's the whole magic.

## The simple habit: "volume first"

At each meal, **start with the high-volume, low-density stuff**:

- Fill **half your plate with vegetables or salad** first.
- Add a **palm of protein** (fish, eggs, chicken, legumes).
- Then a smaller amount of the dense stuff (rice, bread, potatoes, oil, cheese) — to taste, not to
  fill up.

You end the meal full and satisfied, not stuffed, and you've spent far fewer calories than a plate
built the other way around.

> **Try it once and watch:** the night you eat a giant vegetable-and-protein plate, notice how you
> *don't* go hunting in the cupboard at 10 p.m. That's not willpower. That's volume doing the work
> for you.
